Natural Language Processing

Natural language processing (NLP) is a subfield of linguistics, computer science, and artificial
intelligence concerned with the interactions between computers and human language, in particular
how to program computers to process and analyze large amounts of natural language data.

The goal is a computer capable of "understanding" the contents of documents, including the
contextual nuances of the language within them. The technology can then accurately extract
information and insights contained in the documents, as well as categorize and organize the
documents themselves.

Tokenization

Tokenization is the process of breaking text into individual tokens (words, subwords, or characters).
Word tokenization splits text at whitespace and punctuation. Subword tokenization, used by models
like BPE (Byte Pair Encoding) and WordPiece, handles out-of-vocabulary words better by splitting
rare words into more frequent subword units.

Word Embeddings

Word embeddings are dense vector representations of words that capture semantic meaning.
Words with similar meanings have similar vector representations.

Word2Vec: predicts words from their context (CBOW) or context from words (Skip-gram).
GloVe (Global Vectors): learns word vectors from global word-word co-occurrence statistics.
FastText: extends Word2Vec to use subword information, handling morphological variations.
BERT embeddings are context-dependent, producing different vectors for the same word in
different contexts, unlike the static embeddings above.

Language Models

A language model assigns probabilities to sequences of words. Early n-gram models estimated
the probability of a word based on the previous N-1 words. Neural language models use
recurrent networks or transformers to capture longer-range dependencies.

Recurrent Neural Networks for NLP

RNNs process sequences by maintaining a hidden state that captures information about previous
tokens. Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks address the vanishing gradient problem with
gating mechanisms: the forget gate controls what to discard, the input gate what to add,
and the output gate what to output.

GRU (Gated Recurrent Unit) is a simplified version of LSTM with fewer parameters.
Bidirectional RNNs process sequences in both directions, capturing both past and future context.

Transformers

The Transformer architecture, introduced in "Attention Is All You Need" (Vaswani et al., 2017),
uses self-attention mechanisms to process sequences in parallel, enabling better capture of
long-range dependencies and more efficient training.

Self-attention allows each token to attend to all other tokens in the sequence, computing a
weighted sum of their representations based on relevance. Multi-head attention runs multiple
attention operations in parallel to capture different types of relationships.

Positional encoding provides position information to the model since transformers have no
inherent notion of sequence order. Absolute position encodings add fixed or learned vectors;
relative position encodings (like in T5 and GPT-NeoX) encode distances between tokens.

BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) pretrains a transformer encoder
using masked language modeling (predict masked words) and next sentence prediction. Fine-tuning
on downstream tasks achieves state-of-the-art performance on text classification, NER, QA.

GPT (Generative Pretrained Transformer) pretrains an autoregressive decoder that predicts the
next token given previous tokens. GPT-3 and GPT-4 demonstrate remarkable few-shot learning.

Text Classification

Text classification assigns predefined categories to text. Approaches:
- Bag of words + logistic regression: simple but effective baseline
- LSTM or CNN: captures local and sequential patterns
- BERT fine-tuning: state-of-the-art for most classification tasks

Named Entity Recognition (NER)

NER identifies and classifies named entities (persons, organizations, locations, dates) in text.
It is typically formulated as a sequence labeling task using IOB tags (Inside-Outside-Beginning).

Question Answering

Question answering systems either retrieve answers from documents (extractive QA) or generate
them (generative QA). SQuAD is a benchmark for extractive QA where models highlight answer
spans in passages. BERT-based models achieve superhuman performance on SQuAD.

Machine Translation

Neural machine translation uses encoder-decoder architectures with attention. The encoder
processes the source sentence; the decoder generates the target sentence, attending to relevant
source words. Transformer-based systems like Marian MT achieve very high translation quality.

Text Generation

Language models can generate coherent, relevant text when given a prompt. Decoding strategies:
- Greedy decoding: always choose the most likely next token
- Beam search: explore multiple candidate sequences simultaneously
- Top-k sampling: randomly sample from the top k most likely tokens
- Top-p (nucleus) sampling: sample from tokens whose cumulative probability exceeds p
- Temperature: controls randomness (lower = more deterministic)
